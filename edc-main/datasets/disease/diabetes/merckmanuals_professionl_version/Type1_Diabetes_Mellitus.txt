Self-measurement of urine ketones is recommended for patients with type 1 diabetes if patients with type 1 diabetes experience sustained hyperglycemia greater than 250 to 300 mg/dL during glucose self-monitoring.
Urine glucose monitoring is inaccurate and not recommended for patients with type 1 diabetes.
Most long-term complications that occur in patients with type 1 diabetes are the vascular, neurologic, and immunologic complications of diabetes.
Some long-term complications that occur in patients with type 1 diabetes are related to autoimmune comorbidities specific to type 1 diabetes.
Screening recommendations exist for both general complications of diabetes and specific complications of type 1 diabetes.
Acute complications of type 1 diabetes, including diabetic ketoacidosis and hypoglycemia, are discussed elsewhere.
Chronic hyperglycemia leads to complications that affect small vessels, large vessels, or both in patients with type 1 diabetes.
Microvascular complications of type 1 diabetes include retinopathy, nephropathy, and neuropathy.
Neuropathy is a heterogeneous long-term complication of type 1 diabetes, with multifactorial pathogenesis that includes toxic effects of hyperglycemia and advanced glycation end-products on nerves, and ischemia to nerves from microvascular disease of type 1 diabetes.
Macrovascular complications of type 1 diabetes include atherosclerosis of large vessels leading to angina pectoris and myocardial infarction, transient ischemic attacks and strokes, and peripheral artery disease.
Immune dysfunction is another major long-term complication of type 1 diabetes.
Risk of complications of type 1 diabetes can be decreased by strict glycemic control and by management of hypertension and lipid levels in patients with type 1 diabetes.
Specific measures for prevention of progression of complications of type 1 diabetes once detected are described separately under Long-Term Complications of type 1 diabetes.
Patients with type 1 diabetes are at risk for other autoimmune diseases, including thyroid disease, celiac disease, and pernicious anemia.
Thyroid disease is one of the most common autoimmune diseases associated with type 1 diabetes.
Celiac disease is one of the most common autoimmune diseases associated with type 1 diabetes.
Pernicious anemia is one of the most common autoimmune diseases associated with type 1 diabetes.
Addison disease is less commonly associated with type 1 diabetes.
Autoimmune liver disease is less commonly associated with type 1 diabetes.
Myasthenia gravis is less commonly associated with type 1 diabetes.
Adults with type 1 diabetes should undergo screening for retinopathy, a complication of type 1 diabetes, through dilated and comprehensive eye examination within 5 years of diagnosis of type 1 diabetes.
Adults with type 1 diabetes should undergo screening for retinopathy, a complication of type 1 diabetes, every 1 to 2 years after the initial screening.
Patients with diabetes mellitus require foot examination with pulses, reflexes, temperature, pinprick sensation, vibration, and monofilament testing for neuropathy and foot ulcers within 5 years of diabetes diagnosis, and then at least annually.
Patients with diabetes mellitus require spot urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio and estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) testing for nephropathy within 5 years of diabetes diagnosis, and then annually.
Patients with hypertension require blood pressure measurement at every medical visit.
Patients with atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease require lipid profile testing soon after diabetes diagnosis, and then annually.
Patients with atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease may require additional screening depending on signs, symptoms, and additional risk factors.
Patients with heart failure may require measurement of a natriuretic peptide.
Patients with peripheral artery disease who are 65 years or older and have microvascular disease, foot complications, or end-organ damage may require ankle-brachial index testing.
Patients with autoimmune thyroid disease require thyroid function tests soon after diabetes diagnosis.
Patients with celiac disease and symptoms such as diarrhea, malabsorption, or abdominal pain require testing.
Patients with pernicious anemia and symptoms such as peripheral neuropathy or unexplained anemia require testing.
Additional cardiovascular screening for patients with diabetes mellitus may include risk stratification, electrocardiography, or other testing.
Screening for microvascular complications in children with diabetes mellitus begins once the child has reached puberty or age 10 years, whichever is earlier, and 5 years after diabetes diagnosis.
Children and adolescents with type 1 diabetes require specific screening recommendations similar to those for adults with type 1 diabetes.
